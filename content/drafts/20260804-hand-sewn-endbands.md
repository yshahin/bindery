# Hand-sewn Endbands

Most commercial hardcovers usually have a decorative piece of fabric glued to the top and bottom of the spine. These are "stuck-on" endbands, and they are purely cosmetic. A *hand-sewn* endband, however, is a structural component that reinforces the head and tail of the book against the strain of being pulled off a shelf.

### The Structure

A sewn endband consists of:
1.  **The Core:** A round cord (leather, vellum, or cord) that sits on the top edge of the text block.
2.  **The Sewing:** Silk or linen thread that wraps around the core and is tied down into the signatures of the book.

### Why Sew Them?
*   **Strength:** The tie-downs anchor the endband into the book block, helping to maintain the spine's shape (the "arch").
*   **Protection:** The core protects the vulnerable edges of the spine from crushing.
*   **Beauty:** The intricate weaving of colored threads is a hallmark of fine binding.

### Materials
*   **Thread:** Silk buttonhole twist is traditional for its sheen, but embroidery floss works for beginners.
*   **Core:** 1mm - 2mm round leather cord or stiff hemp cord.
*   **Needle:** A small, sharp embroidery needle.

### Basic Technique: Two-Color Pattern

1.  **Anchor:** Start in the center of the first signature. Bring the thread out through the spine fold, just below the kettle stitch.
2.  **Wrap:** Position the core on the text block edge. Wrap the thread over the core.
3.  **Tie-Down:** Every few wraps (or every signature), the needle goes back into the book spine (a few millimeters down) and comes out the back of the spine to anchor the loop.

### Common Styles
*   **Single Core:** The standard round band.
*   **Double Core:** A smaller core sits in front of a larger one (typical in French bindings).
*   **Flat Coptic:** Sewn into the covers of Coptic bindings, often extending across the spine.

Practicing endbands is excellent training for tension control and fine needlework.
